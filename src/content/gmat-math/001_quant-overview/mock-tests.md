# Mock Exams & Practice Test Sources

Full-length practice tests (CATs — Computer Adaptive Tests) are the single highest-leverage tool in GMAT prep. They diagnose weaknesses, build stamina, and calibrate timing. This page lists the most trusted external sources and how to use them.

## Why mocks matter

- **Diagnostic** — your first mock reveals where the gap actually is, not where you think it is.
- **Stamina** — the real Quant section is 45 minutes of dense problem solving. You need to *train* that endurance, not just learn content.
- **Pacing** — ~2 min 9 sec per question. The only way to internalize that rhythm is timed practice.
- **Adaptive feel** — the GMAT Focus exam is computer adaptive. Quality mocks simulate this; static PDFs do not.

## Recommended cadence

| Phase | When | Source |
|-------|------|--------|
| Diagnostic | Week 1 | 1 free official mock (GMAC) |
| Mid-prep checkpoints | Every 2–3 weeks | Manhattan / GMAT Club / Target Test Prep |
| Final ramp | Last 3 weeks | Remaining 5 official GMAC mocks, 1 per ~4 days |

Save the official GMAC mocks for the back half — they are the most accurate score predictors. Burning them early wastes the signal.

## Sources

### Official (most accurate)
- **GMAC Official Practice Exams** — `mba.com`. Six exams total: 2 free, 4 paid. Same scoring algorithm as the real test. The only mocks whose scores you should trust as a real-test prediction.

### Free diagnostics (one each)
- **Kaplan Free GMAT Practice Test** — solid first diagnostic if you've already used your free official mocks.
- **Veritas Prep Free Practice Test** — slightly harder than the real exam.
- **Princeton Review Free Practice Test** — gentler difficulty curve; good early.

### Subscription / paid
- **GMAT Club Tests** — the largest community-vetted question bank; CATs and quizzes. Free access through forum activity, otherwise paid.
- **Manhattan Prep CATs** — six exams, sold as a bundle. Notoriously *harder* than the real test, especially Quant. Great ceiling training; bad final-week confidence builder.
- **Target Test Prep** — Quant-specialized adaptive course with strong analytics. Best for students whose weak section is Quant specifically.
- **Magoosh GMAT** — subscription with video explanations for every question. Strong for self-study learners.
- **Experts' Global** — 20-mock bundle. Volume play for stamina-building.

## Difficulty calibration

This matters more than students realize:

| Source | Relative to real GMAT Focus |
|--------|----------------------------|
| GMAC Official | ≈ real exam |
| Manhattan Prep | Harder (especially Quant) |
| Target Test Prep | Slightly harder |
| Veritas Prep | Slightly harder |
| Kaplan | ≈ real exam |
| Princeton Review | Slightly easier |

If your Manhattan score is 30 points below your official-mock score, that is **normal**, not a regression.

## How to debrief a mock

The mock is worthless without the post-mortem. After every full-length:

1. **Don't look at the score first.** Look at the errors.
2. **Categorize each miss**: content gap (didn't know), execution error (knew it, miscalculated), time-pressure error (rushed), trap (fell for distractor).
3. **Maintain an error log**: question stem → your answer → correct answer → category → one-line "what I should have done."
4. **Re-derive every wrong answer from scratch** before reading the explanation. This is where retention compounds.
5. **Track patterns weekly.** Three weighted-average mistakes across two mocks is a content lesson to redo. Three timing errors in the last 7 questions is a pacing problem, not a content one.

A 90-minute debrief on a 2-hour mock is normal. A 10-minute debrief is wasted prep.
