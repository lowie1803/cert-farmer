# Interest, Profit & Discount

## Simple Interest

$$I = P \times r \times t$$

- P = principal (starting amount)
- r = annual interest rate (as decimal)
- t = time in years
- Total amount = P + I = P(1 + rt)

> $1,000 at 5% simple interest for 3 years.
I = 1000 × 0.05 × 3 = **$150**
Total = $1,150

## Compound Interest

$$A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

- A = final amount
- r = annual rate
- n = compounds per year (1=annual, 2=semi-annual, 12=monthly)
- t = years

For annual compounding (n=1): **A = P(1 + r)ᵗ**

> $500 at 10% compounded annually for 2 years.
A = 500 × (1.10)² = 500 × 1.21 = **$605**

Simple interest would give: 500 + 500×0.10×2 = **$600** — compound interest always earns more.

## Profit & Loss

**Profit** = Selling Price − Cost Price  
**Loss** = Cost Price − Selling Price

$$\text{Profit \%} = \frac{\text{Profit}}{\text{Cost Price}} \times 100$$

> Bought for $80, sold for $100.
Profit = $20. Profit % = 20/80 × 100 = **25%**

> Bought for $150, sold for $120.
Loss = $30. Loss % = 30/150 × 100 = **20%**

**Key**: The base for profit/loss percent is always the **cost price**, not the selling price.

## Markup

**Markup** is added to cost to get selling price:

$$\text{Selling Price} = \text{Cost} \times (1 + \text{markup \%})$$

> Cost = $60, markup = 40%.
SP = 60 × 1.40 = **$84**

## Discount

**Discount** is subtracted from the marked (original) price:

$$\text{Sale Price} = \text{Marked Price} \times (1 - \text{discount \%})$$

> Original $200, 30% discount.
Sale price = 200 × 0.70 = **$140**

### Successive Discounts

Multiply the multipliers — don't add the percentages:

> 20% off, then 10% more off.
Final = P × 0.80 × 0.90 = 0.72P → **28% total discount**, not 30%.

### Reverse Discount (finding original price)

> After 15% discount, an item costs $85. Original price?
0.85 × Original = 85 → Original = 85/0.85 = **$100**

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Compound vs. simple
$1,000 at 8% for 2 years.
- Simple: 1000 × 0.08 × 2 = $160 interest
- Compound: 1000 × 1.08² = 1000 × 1.1664 = $1,166.40 → **$166.40 interest**

### Example 2: Markup then discount
Cost = $100, markup 50%, then 33.3% discount:
- After markup: $150
- After discount: 150 × 0.667 = ~$100
Selling at a loss of essentially $0 (coincidence of numbers here).

### Example 3: Finding how long for target interest
At what rate does $5,000 need to be invested (simple interest) to earn $1,200 in 4 years?
1200 = 5000 × r × 4 → r = 1200/20000 = **6%**

## Common Traps

- **Profit % base**: Always cost price, not selling price.
- **"25% profit on selling price"** vs. **"25% profit on cost"** — these are different.
- **Compound interest grows faster**: Don't use the simple interest formula when the problem says "compounded."
- **Successive discounts**: 20% off + 10% off ≠ 30% off. Multiply the multipliers.
