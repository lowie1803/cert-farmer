# Pinyin: Initials & Finals

Pinyin (拼音 *pīnyīn*) is the romanization the whole HSK exam is taught through. It is **not** English — every letter is a contract about a specific Mandarin sound. Spend the time now; every later week pays interest on it.

A pinyin syllable = **initial** (consonant onset, optional) + **final** (vowel core + optional nasal coda) + **tone** (marked on the main vowel).

## Initials (21 of them)

| Group | Letters | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Stops (unaspirated / aspirated) | b/p, d/t, g/k | Mandarin contrast is **aspiration**, not voicing. `b` is closer to English unvoiced *p* than to *b*. |
| Affricates | z/c, zh/ch, j/q | `z` ≈ *ds*; `c` ≈ *ts* (strong puff). `zh/ch` are retroflex (tongue curled back). `j/q` are palatal (tongue near hard palate). |
| Fricatives | s, sh, x, h, f | `s` flat; `sh` retroflex; `x` palatal; `h` is throaty like German *ich-laut*. |
| Nasals & laterals | m, n, l, r | `r` is a retroflex glide, **not** rolled. |

The **only** truly tricky contrasts for English speakers:

- `zh / ch / sh / r` (retroflex — tongue curled, tip points up)
- `j / q / x` (palatal — tongue flat, tip behind lower teeth)
- `z / c / s` (alveolar — tip at upper teeth)

These three rows are mutually exclusive. If you mix them, native speakers can usually recover, but it ruins listening — you can't hear what you can't pronounce.

## Finals (~35)

Finals are the *meaningful* part of the syllable. Memorize the chart in groups:

| Simple | Compound | Nasal coda -n | Nasal coda -ng |
|--------|----------|---------------|----------------|
| a, o, e, i, u, ü | ai, ei, ao, ou | an, en, in, un, ün | ang, eng, ing, ong |
| | ia, ie, iao, iou | ian, uan, üan | iang, uang, iong |
| | ua, uo, uai, uei | | ueng |

Spelling shortcuts to **memorize once and stop tripping on**:

- `i` after `z c s zh ch sh r` is **not** the *ee* sound — it's a buzzed continuation of the consonant (`sī`, `chī`). Treat it as a separate vowel.
- `u` after `j q x y` is actually `ü` (written without the umlaut): `ju, qu, xu, yu` = `jü, qü, xü, yü`.
- `iou → iu`, `uei → ui`, `uen → un` after an initial: `liù, guī, lùn`. The dropped letter is still pronounced.

## Syllable-final habits

There are **no consonant clusters** and **no final stops** in Mandarin. A syllable always ends in a vowel, `-n`, `-ng`, or the retroflex `-r` suffix. If you find yourself ending a syllable on `-t`, `-k`, `-s`, you're speaking English.
