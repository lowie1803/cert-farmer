# Tones & Tone-3 Sandhi

Mandarin has **four lexical tones** plus a neutral tone. The same syllable in two different tones is a different word. `mā / má / mǎ / mà` = mother / hemp / horse / scold. This is not optional decoration.

## The four tones

| Tone | Mark | Shape | Mental image |
|------|------|-------|--------------|
| 1 | ā | High, flat, sustained | A doctor asking you to say "aaah" |
| 2 | á | Rising, mid → high | The questioning "huh?" |
| 3 | ǎ | Low dip (mostly low, slight rise at end in isolation) | A skeptical "weeell..." |
| 4 | à | Sharp falling, high → low | An angry "No!" |
| neutral | a | Short, unstressed, takes pitch from the previous syllable | The unstressed *a* in *sofa* |

**The single biggest beginner mistake:** treating tone 3 as a falling-then-rising dip in connected speech. In isolation it does dip and rise. In a sentence, tone 3 is almost always pronounced as a **low tone** (half-third). Don't sing it.

## Tone-3 sandhi (the only mandatory tone change in HSK 1)

When **two tone-3 syllables come in a row**, the first one shifts to **tone 2**.

- 你好 is written `nǐ hǎo` but pronounced `ní hǎo`.
- 很好 written `hěn hǎo`, pronounced `hén hǎo`.
- 我也很好 → 我 (3→2) 也 (3→2) 很 (3→2) 好 (3, stays).

Three-in-a-row: usually the first two become tone 2, the last stays tone 3. The dictionary still spells everything with `ˇ` — the change is a pronunciation rule, not a spelling rule.

## 不 and 一 — two special sandhi words

- **不 (`bù`)** is tone 4 by default, but becomes **tone 2** before another tone-4 syllable: `bù shì` → `bú shì`.
- **一 (`yī`)** changes a lot:
  - Counting/standalone: `yī` (tone 1).
  - Before tone 4: `yí` (tone 2). 一个 → `yí gè`.
  - Before tones 1/2/3: `yì` (tone 4). 一年 → `yì nián`.

Don't memorize these as exceptions — say them out loud enough times that your mouth refuses to do the unnatural version.

## Neutral tone

Neutral-tone syllables (de, le, ma, ne, ba, particle 了, suffix -子, second syllable of many disyllabic words like 妈妈 *māma*) are **short and unstressed**. Their pitch is dictated by the preceding tone. They are not "tone 0" — they are *no tone*, and the duration cue matters as much as pitch.

---

**Practice prompt:** drill the four-tone *mā/má/mǎ/mà* contrast until you can produce it without thinking, then do the same with *yī/yí/yǐ/yì* and *bā/bá/bǎ/bà*. If a friend can hear which one you said, you're ready for week 2.
