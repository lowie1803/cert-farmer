# Major Stock Exchanges

## What is a Stock Exchange?

A stock exchange is a regulated marketplace where buyers and sellers trade stocks. Think of it as a supermarket for securities — it provides the infrastructure, rules, and oversight for fair trading.

## The Big Two in the US

### NYSE (New York Stock Exchange)
- **Founded**: 1792
- **Location**: Wall Street, New York City
- **Listed companies**: ~2,400
- **Total market cap**: ~$25 trillion
- **Style**: Hybrid — electronic trading + designated market makers on the floor
- **Notable listings**: Berkshire Hathaway, Walmart, JPMorgan, Coca-Cola

### NASDAQ
- **Founded**: 1971
- **Location**: Fully electronic (no physical trading floor)
- **Listed companies**: ~3,700
- **Total market cap**: ~$22 trillion
- **Style**: 100% electronic
- **Notable listings**: Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, Tesla

## Key Differences

| Feature | NYSE | NASDAQ |
|---------|------|--------|
| Trading style | Hybrid (floor + electronic) | Fully electronic |
| Reputation | Traditional, blue-chip | Tech-heavy, growth |
| Listing fees | Higher | Lower |
| Market makers | Designated (1 per stock) | Multiple competing |

## Stock Market Indexes

An **index** tracks the performance of a group of stocks:

- **S&P 500** — 500 largest US companies (the most-watched benchmark)
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)** — 30 major US companies
- **NASDAQ Composite** — all NASDAQ-listed stocks (~3,700)
- **Russell 2000** — 2,000 small-cap US companies

## Key Takeaway

> NYSE and NASDAQ dominate US trading. Indexes like the S&P 500 let you track the overall market at a glance.
