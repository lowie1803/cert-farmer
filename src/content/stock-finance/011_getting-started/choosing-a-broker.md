# Choosing a Broker

## What is a Brokerage Account?

A **brokerage account** is where you buy and sell investments. Think of it as a bank account, but instead of just holding cash, it lets you purchase stocks, ETFs, bonds, and other securities.

## Types of Accounts

### Individual Brokerage Account (Taxable)
- No contribution limits
- No restrictions on withdrawals
- Gains are taxed (capital gains tax)
- Most flexible option

### Retirement Accounts (Tax-Advantaged)

| Account | Key Features |
|---------|-------------|
| **401(k)** | Employer-sponsored, often with matching. Pre-tax contributions. Penalty for early withdrawal before 59½ |
| **Traditional IRA** | Tax-deductible contributions. Taxed on withdrawal. $7,000/year limit (2024) |
| **Roth IRA** | Contributions with after-tax money. Withdrawals are tax-free. Same $7,000 limit |

**Rule of thumb**: If your employer offers 401(k) matching, contribute at least enough to get the full match — it's free money.

## What to Look For in a Broker

### 1. Commission and Fees
- Most major brokers now offer **$0 commission** on stock and ETF trades
- Watch for: account maintenance fees, transfer fees, options fees, mutual fund fees

### 2. Account Minimums
- Many brokers have **$0 minimum** to open an account
- Some require $500–$3,000 for certain account types

### 3. Investment Selection
- Stocks and ETFs: Available everywhere
- Mutual funds: Selection varies by broker
- Bonds, options, crypto: Not all brokers offer these

### 4. Research & Tools
- Stock screeners, analyst reports, charting tools
- Educational resources (articles, videos, courses)
- Important for beginners learning to research

### 5. User Experience
- Mobile app quality matters if you'll invest from your phone
- Easy-to-understand interface vs. professional-grade complexity
- Customer support availability

### 6. Account Security
- SIPC insurance (protects up to $500,000 if the brokerage fails)
- Two-factor authentication
- Reputation and regulatory compliance

## Getting Started Checklist

1. Decide: taxable account, IRA, or both
2. Compare 2-3 brokers on fees and features
3. Open account online (takes 10-15 minutes)
4. Link your bank account
5. Transfer funds
6. Start investing!

> **Key takeaway**: For most beginners, any major broker with $0 commissions will work fine. Don't overthink it — the most important step is actually opening the account and starting to invest.
