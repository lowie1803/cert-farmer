# Stocks vs Bonds

## The Two Core Asset Classes

Stocks and bonds are the foundation of most investment portfolios. Understanding the difference is essential.

## Stocks (Equity)

When you buy stock, you **own** part of the company.

- **Return**: Capital gains + dividends
- **Risk**: High — stock prices can drop significantly
- **Upside**: Unlimited potential
- **Income**: Dividends (not guaranteed)
- **Maturity**: None — you hold as long as you want

## Bonds (Debt)

When you buy a bond, you **lend money** to a company or government.

- **Return**: Fixed interest payments (coupons) + return of principal
- **Risk**: Lower — you get paid before stockholders
- **Upside**: Limited to the stated interest rate
- **Income**: Regular coupon payments (guaranteed unless issuer defaults)
- **Maturity**: Fixed — the bond expires and you get your money back

## Head-to-Head Comparison

| Feature | Stocks | Bonds |
|---------|--------|-------|
| You are a... | Part owner | Lender |
| Return type | Variable | Fixed |
| Risk level | Higher | Lower |
| Income | Dividends (variable) | Coupons (fixed) |
| In bankruptcy | Last to be paid | Paid before stockholders |
| Price volatility | High | Low to moderate |
| Long-term returns | ~10% avg/year (S&P 500) | ~4-6% avg/year |

## Types of Bonds

| Type | Issuer | Risk | Example |
|------|--------|------|---------|
| **Treasury bonds** | US Government | Very low (risk-free benchmark) | 10-year T-note |
| **Municipal bonds** | State/local government | Low | Tax-exempt, good for high earners |
| **Corporate bonds** | Companies | Low to high | Investment grade vs junk bonds |
| **High-yield (junk)** | Risky companies | High | Higher interest to compensate |

## Bond Ratings

Credit agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) rate bond safety:

| Rating | Grade | Risk |
|--------|-------|------|
| AAA to BBB- | Investment grade | Low |
| BB+ and below | High yield / Junk | High |

## When to Use Each

- **Stocks**: For long-term growth (5+ years). Accept short-term volatility for higher returns.
- **Bonds**: For stability, income, and capital preservation. Good for shorter time horizons or risk reduction.

## Key Takeaway

> Stocks offer higher returns but more risk (ownership). Bonds offer lower returns but more stability (lending). Most portfolios use both.
