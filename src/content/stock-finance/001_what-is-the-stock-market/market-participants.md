# Market Participants

## Who is in the Stock Market?

The stock market is an ecosystem with many types of participants, each with different goals, strategies, and influence.

## Retail Investors

**You.** Individual investors who buy and sell stocks through brokerage accounts.

- Typically invest smaller amounts
- Use platforms like Robinhood, Fidelity, Interactive Brokers, or Schwab
- Growing rapidly — retail trading volume has surged since 2020

## Institutional Investors

Large organizations that invest massive amounts of money:

| Type | Example | Strategy |
|------|---------|----------|
| Mutual funds | Vanguard, Fidelity | Pool money from many investors |
| Hedge funds | Bridgewater, Citadel | Aggressive strategies, high fees |
| Pension funds | CalPERS | Long-term, conservative |
| Insurance companies | Berkshire Hathaway | Invest premiums collected |
| Sovereign wealth funds | Norway's GPFG | Government-owned, massive scale |

Institutional investors control roughly **80% of stock market volume**.

## Market Makers

Firms that provide **liquidity** by always being ready to buy and sell. They profit from the **spread** — the small difference between the buy price (bid) and the sell price (ask).

Example: A market maker might offer to buy AAPL at $174.50 and sell at $174.52. The $0.02 spread is their profit.

## Brokers

Intermediaries that execute trades on your behalf. Modern discount brokers charge $0 commission on stock trades (they make money from payment for order flow, interest on cash, etc.).

## Regulators

- **SEC** (Securities and Exchange Commission) — protects investors, enforces rules
- **FINRA** — regulates broker-dealers
- **Federal Reserve** — sets interest rates, which heavily influence stock prices

## Key Takeaway

> The market has many players with different roles. As a retail investor, understanding who you're trading alongside helps you make better decisions.
