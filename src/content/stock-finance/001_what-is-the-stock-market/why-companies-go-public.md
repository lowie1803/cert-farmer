# Why Companies Go Public

## Private vs Public Companies

- **Private company**: Owned by founders, employees, and private investors. Shares are not available to the general public (e.g., SpaceX, Valve).
- **Public company**: Shares are listed on a stock exchange and anyone can buy them (e.g., Apple, Tesla, Google).

## The IPO Process

An **Initial Public Offering (IPO)** is when a private company sells shares to the public for the first time.

### Steps:
1. **Hire investment banks** (underwriters) to manage the process
2. **File with regulators** (SEC in the US) — disclose financials, risks, business model
3. **Set a price range** — underwriters gauge investor demand (roadshow)
4. **IPO day** — shares begin trading on an exchange
5. **Post-IPO** — the stock trades freely; the company is now public

## Why Go Public?

| Reason | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Raise capital | Sell shares to fund growth, R&D, acquisitions |
| Liquidity | Early investors and employees can sell their shares |
| Prestige | Being publicly listed boosts credibility |
| Currency | Public stock can be used to acquire other companies |

## The Tradeoffs

Going public also means:
- **Regulatory burden** — quarterly earnings reports, audits, SEC filings
- **Public scrutiny** — anyone can see your financials
- **Short-term pressure** — investors may push for quarterly results over long-term vision
- **Loss of control** — shareholders have voting rights

## Recent Trends

- **Direct listings** — skip the underwriters, list shares directly (e.g., Spotify, Coinbase)
- **SPACs** — a shell company IPOs first, then merges with a private company to take it public

## Key Takeaway

> Companies go public mainly to raise money and provide liquidity. The trade-off is increased regulation and public accountability.
