# Dollar Cost Averaging

## What is DCA?

**Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA)** is investing a **fixed amount of money at regular intervals**, regardless of the stock price.

Instead of trying to time the perfect entry point, you invest consistently — every week, every two weeks, or every month.

## How It Works

Say you invest **$500 per month** in an S&P 500 index fund:

| Month | Price | Shares Bought |
|-------|-------|--------------|
| January | $50 | 10.0 shares |
| February | $40 | 12.5 shares |
| March | $45 | 11.1 shares |
| April | $55 | 9.1 shares |
| May | $50 | 10.0 shares |

**Total invested**: $2,500
**Total shares**: 52.7
**Average cost per share**: $47.44

Notice your average cost ($47.44) is **lower** than the simple average price ($48.00). That's because you automatically bought **more shares when prices were low** and fewer when prices were high.

## Why DCA Works

### 1. Removes Emotion
- No agonizing over "is now the right time?"
- You invest mechanically, avoiding fear and greed

### 2. Reduces Timing Risk
- Nobody can consistently predict market tops and bottoms
- DCA spreads your risk across many entry points

### 3. Builds Discipline
- Regular investing becomes a habit
- Works perfectly with automatic paycheck deductions (401k, automatic transfers)

### 4. Takes Advantage of Dips
- When prices drop, your fixed amount buys more shares
- Volatility becomes your friend, not your enemy

## DCA vs Lump Sum Investing

What if you have $12,000 — should you invest it all now or spread it over 12 months?

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **Lump sum** | Higher expected returns (markets go up ~70% of the time) | Risk of investing right before a drop |
| **DCA** | Lower risk, less anxiety | May miss early gains in a rising market |

Studies show lump sum investing wins about **2/3 of the time** because markets tend to go up. But DCA wins on **peace of mind** — and a strategy you can stick with beats one you abandon.

## How to Implement DCA

1. **Pick your amount**: What can you invest each period? Even $50/month works
2. **Pick your interval**: Monthly is most common (aligns with paychecks)
3. **Pick your investment**: A broad index fund (S&P 500, total market) is ideal
4. **Automate it**: Set up automatic transfers so you don't have to think about it
5. **Don't stop during dips**: This is when DCA helps you most

## When DCA Doesn't Make Sense

- If you have a small lump sum and a very long time horizon → just invest it
- For short-term savings goals (< 2 years) → keep in savings
- If you're trying to trade actively → DCA is a passive strategy

> **Key takeaway**: DCA is the simplest, most beginner-friendly investment strategy. It won't maximize returns in theory, but it maximizes the chance that you **actually invest consistently** — which matters more than perfect timing.
