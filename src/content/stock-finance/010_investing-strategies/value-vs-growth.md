# Value vs Growth Investing

## Two Schools of Thought

The stock market has two dominant investing philosophies. Understanding both helps you choose an approach — or combine them.

## Value Investing

**Buy stocks that are trading below their true worth**, then wait for the market to recognize the value.

### The Philosophy
- The market sometimes **misprices** stocks due to fear, bad news, or neglect
- If you buy undervalued companies with strong fundamentals, you'll profit when the price corrects
- Pioneered by **Benjamin Graham** and perfected by **Warren Buffett**

### What Value Investors Look For
| Metric | Value Signal |
|--------|-------------|
| Low P/E ratio | Paying less per dollar of earnings |
| Low P/B ratio | Stock price below book value |
| High dividend yield | Regular income while you wait |
| Strong balance sheet | Low debt, plenty of cash |
| Margin of safety | Buying well below estimated intrinsic value |

### Examples of Value Stocks
Mature, established companies in "boring" industries: banks, utilities, consumer staples, insurance.

### Pros & Cons
- **Pro**: Lower risk, backed by fundamentals, proven long-term track record
- **Con**: Can be slow — "value traps" where cheap stocks stay cheap or get cheaper

## Growth Investing

**Buy stocks of companies growing revenue and earnings rapidly**, even if the current price looks expensive.

### The Philosophy
- Fast-growing companies will eventually grow into (and beyond) their high valuations
- Pay a premium now for outsized future returns
- Focus on **revenue growth, market opportunity, and innovation**

### What Growth Investors Look For
| Metric | Growth Signal |
|--------|-------------|
| Revenue growth >20%/year | Rapidly expanding business |
| Large addressable market | Room to grow for years |
| Competitive advantages | Technology, network effects, brand |
| High R&D spending | Investing in future products |
| Visionary management | Track record of execution |

### Examples of Growth Stocks
Technology, biotech, innovative disruptors: companies like Amazon, Tesla, and Nvidia were classic growth stocks.

### Pros & Cons
- **Pro**: Potential for massive returns, exciting companies
- **Con**: Higher risk, expensive valuations, sensitive to interest rates, many growth stocks fail

## Value vs Growth: Historical Performance

| Period | Winner |
|--------|--------|
| 2000–2007 | Value (after dot-com crash punished growth) |
| 2008–2020 | Growth (tech boom, low interest rates) |
| 2022 | Value (rising rates crushed growth valuations) |
| Long-term (50+ years) | Roughly equal, with value having a slight edge |

## The Blend Approach

Many successful investors combine both:
- **Core**: Index funds (mix of value and growth)
- **Satellite**: Individual value or growth picks you have conviction in

> **Key takeaway**: Neither approach is universally "better." Value tends to outperform in bear markets; growth tends to outperform in bull markets. Your choice depends on your risk tolerance, time horizon, and conviction.
