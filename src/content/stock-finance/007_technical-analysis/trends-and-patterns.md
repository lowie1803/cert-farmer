# Trends & Patterns

## What is a Trend?

A **trend** is the general direction a stock's price is moving over time. Identifying trends is the foundation of technical analysis.

### Three Types of Trends

| Trend | Description | Characteristics |
|-------|------------|-----------------|
| **Uptrend** | Price making higher highs and higher lows | Bullish — buyers in control |
| **Downtrend** | Price making lower highs and lower lows | Bearish — sellers in control |
| **Sideways** | Price bouncing between a range | Consolidation — no clear direction |

## Trendlines

A **trendline** is a straight line drawn on a chart connecting price points:

- **Uptrend line**: Connect two or more **higher lows** — acts as support
- **Downtrend line**: Connect two or more **lower highs** — acts as resistance

The more times price touches a trendline without breaking it, the **stronger** the trend.

## Common Chart Patterns

### Reversal Patterns (signal trend change)

**Head & Shoulders**
- Three peaks: left shoulder, head (highest), right shoulder
- The "neckline" connects the lows between peaks
- When price breaks below the neckline → bearish reversal
- Inverse head & shoulders = bullish reversal

**Double Top / Double Bottom**
- **Double top**: Price hits resistance twice, then falls — bearish
- **Double bottom**: Price hits support twice, then rises — bullish
- Shaped like the letters "M" (top) and "W" (bottom)

### Continuation Patterns (signal trend will continue)

**Cup & Handle**
- U-shaped recovery (cup) followed by a small pullback (handle)
- Bullish pattern — suggests price will break higher

**Flags & Pennants**
- Short consolidation after a sharp move
- **Flag**: Small rectangle against the trend
- **Pennant**: Small triangle after a sharp move
- Both suggest the trend will resume

**Triangles**
- **Ascending**: Flat top, rising bottom — usually bullish
- **Descending**: Falling top, flat bottom — usually bearish
- **Symmetrical**: Converging lines — could break either way

## Key Takeaway

> No pattern works 100% of the time. Patterns show **probabilities**, not certainties. Always use them alongside other analysis tools and risk management.
