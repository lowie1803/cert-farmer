# Types of Risk

## All Investing Involves Risk

There's no such thing as a risk-free investment that also generates meaningful returns. Understanding the **types** of risk helps you manage them effectively.

## Market Risk (Systematic Risk)

The risk that the **entire market** declines — recessions, pandemics, financial crises.

- Affects virtually all stocks
- **Cannot** be diversified away
- Example: In March 2020, the S&P 500 dropped ~34% in about a month due to COVID-19

### How to manage it:
- Maintain a long time horizon
- Keep bonds/cash as a buffer
- Don't panic sell

## Company Risk (Unsystematic Risk)

The risk specific to an **individual company** — bad management, product failure, lawsuits, fraud.

- Example: A pharmaceutical company's key drug fails clinical trials → stock drops 50%
- **Can be diversified away** by owning many stocks

### How to manage it:
- Diversify across many companies
- Use index funds for instant diversification

## Inflation Risk

The risk that your returns don't keep up with **rising prices**.

- If inflation is 4% and your investment returns 3%, you're **losing** purchasing power
- Cash and low-yield bonds are most vulnerable

### How to manage it:
- Hold growth assets (stocks) that tend to outpace inflation
- Consider TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)

## Interest Rate Risk

When interest rates rise, **bond prices fall** (and vice versa).

- Also affects stocks: higher rates make borrowing more expensive for companies
- Growth/tech stocks are especially sensitive to rate changes

### How to manage it:
- Hold shorter-duration bonds (less sensitive)
- Diversify across asset classes

## Liquidity Risk

The risk that you **can't sell** an investment quickly without taking a big price cut.

- Large-cap stocks: Very liquid — easy to buy/sell instantly
- Small-cap stocks: Less liquid
- Real estate: Very illiquid — can take months to sell

### How to manage it:
- Keep some holdings in highly liquid assets
- Don't invest money you might need soon in illiquid assets

## Currency Risk

If you invest internationally, exchange rate fluctuations can affect your returns.

- Example: Your Japanese stock gains 10% in yen, but the yen drops 8% vs. the dollar → your real return is only ~2%

### How to manage it:
- Be aware of currency exposure in international investments
- Some funds offer currency-hedged versions

## Concentration Risk

Having too much of your portfolio in a **single stock, sector, or asset**.

- Many employees have this risk if their retirement accounts are heavy in company stock
- Example: Enron employees lost both their jobs and their retirement savings

### How to manage it:
- Diversify broadly
- Never have more than 5-10% of your portfolio in a single stock

> **Key takeaway**: You can't eliminate all risk, but you can understand and manage it. The goal isn't zero risk — it's taking the **right amount** of risk for your goals and timeline.
