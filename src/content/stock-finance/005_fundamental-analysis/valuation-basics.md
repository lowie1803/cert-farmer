# Valuation Basics

## The Core Question

> "Is this stock worth its current price?"

Valuation is the process of estimating what a company (or its stock) is **actually worth**, independent of what the market currently says.

## Market Value vs Intrinsic Value

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Market Value** | What the market is currently willing to pay (stock price × shares) |
| **Intrinsic Value** | The "true" value based on the company's fundamentals |

- If **intrinsic > market price** → stock may be **undervalued** (potential buy)
- If **intrinsic < market price** → stock may be **overvalued** (potential sell/avoid)

## Approaches to Valuation

### 1. Relative Valuation (Comparing Multiples)

Compare a company's ratios to peers:

- Stock A has P/E of 15, industry average is 25 → possibly undervalued
- Stock B has P/E of 40, industry average is 20 → possibly overvalued

Common multiples: P/E, P/B, P/S (Price-to-Sales), EV/EBITDA

**Pros**: Simple, quick
**Cons**: Assumes peers are fairly valued

### 2. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) — The Gold Standard

DCF estimates intrinsic value by projecting **future cash flows** and discounting them back to today's value.

The idea: $100 received next year is worth less than $100 today (because of inflation, opportunity cost, and risk).

**Simplified DCF steps**:
1. Estimate future free cash flows for 5-10 years
2. Choose a **discount rate** (typically 8-12%, reflecting risk)
3. Calculate a **terminal value** (value beyond the projection period)
4. Discount everything back to present value
5. Sum it up = intrinsic value

**Pros**: Based on fundamentals, not market sentiment
**Cons**: Highly sensitive to assumptions — small changes in growth rate or discount rate dramatically change the result

### 3. Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

For dividend-paying stocks:

> **Value = Next Year's Dividend ÷ (Required Return − Dividend Growth Rate)**

Only works for mature companies with stable, growing dividends.

## Practical Tips for Beginners

1. **Start with relative valuation** — compare P/E, P/B, and growth rates across competitors
2. **Use multiple methods** — if several approaches suggest a stock is undervalued, the signal is stronger
3. **Be skeptical of precision** — valuation is an art, not an exact science. Think in ranges, not exact numbers
4. **Margin of safety** — even if you calculate intrinsic value at $100, only buy well below that (e.g., at $70-80) to account for errors in your analysis

> **Warren Buffett**: "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."
