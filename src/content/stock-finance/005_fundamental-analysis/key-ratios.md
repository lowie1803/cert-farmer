# Key Financial Ratios

## Why Ratios Matter

Raw numbers on financial statements are hard to compare across companies. A company earning $1 billion sounds impressive — but what if their revenue is $100 billion? Ratios **normalize** the data so you can compare any two companies.

## Valuation Ratios

### P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

The most widely used valuation metric.

> **P/E = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)**

- Example: Stock at $150, EPS of $5 → P/E = 30
- Interpretation: You're paying $30 for every $1 of earnings
- **High P/E** (>25): Investors expect high growth (or stock is overvalued)
- **Low P/E** (<15): Could be undervalued or company has problems

### P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)

> **P/B = Stock Price ÷ Book Value Per Share**

- Book value = Total Assets − Total Liabilities
- **P/B < 1**: Stock is trading below the company's net asset value — potentially undervalued
- **P/B > 3**: Investors are paying a premium for intangible value (brand, growth)

### EPS (Earnings Per Share)

> **EPS = Net Income ÷ Shares Outstanding**

- Example: $10M net income, 2M shares → EPS = $5
- Higher is better; look for **consistent EPS growth** over years
- Compare year-over-year to see if the company is growing profitably

## Profitability Ratios

### ROE (Return on Equity)

> **ROE = Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity × 100%**

- Measures how efficiently the company uses shareholders' money
- **ROE > 15%**: Generally considered good
- Compare within the same industry — some industries naturally have higher ROE

### Profit Margin

> **Profit Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue × 100%**

- Shows what percentage of each dollar of revenue becomes profit
- Software companies: 20-30% margins (high)
- Grocery stores: 1-3% margins (low but normal)

## Financial Health Ratios

### Debt-to-Equity (D/E)

> **D/E = Total Liabilities ÷ Shareholders' Equity**

- **D/E < 1**: Company has more equity than debt — conservative
- **D/E > 2**: Heavily leveraged — higher risk but potentially higher returns
- Compare within industries: utilities normally have high D/E, tech companies low

### Dividend Yield

> **Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend Per Share ÷ Stock Price × 100%**

- Example: $3 dividend, $100 stock → 3% yield
- High yield (>4%) may signal a mature company — or a falling stock price
- Growth companies typically pay little or no dividend

## How to Use Ratios

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Compare ratios within the **same industry** | Compare a tech company's P/E to a utility's |
| Look at ratio **trends over time** | Judge a company on a single quarter |
| Use **multiple ratios together** | Rely on P/E alone |
| Check what's "normal" for the sector | Assume high P/E = bad or low P/E = good |
