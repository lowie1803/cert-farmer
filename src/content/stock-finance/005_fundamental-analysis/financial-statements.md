# Financial Statements

## The Three Core Statements

Every public company must publish financial statements quarterly and annually. There are three main statements you need to understand:

## 1. Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

Shows how much money the company **made or lost** over a period.

| Line Item | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Revenue** (Sales) | Total money earned from selling products/services |
| **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** | Direct costs to produce what was sold |
| **Gross Profit** | Revenue − COGS |
| **Operating Expenses** | Rent, salaries, marketing, R&D |
| **Operating Income** | Gross Profit − Operating Expenses |
| **Net Income** | Final profit after taxes and interest — the "bottom line" |

**Key question**: Is the company profitable, and is profit growing?

## 2. Balance Sheet

A **snapshot** of what the company owns and owes at a specific point in time.

**The fundamental equation**:
> Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

| Section | Examples |
|---------|----------|
| **Assets** | Cash, inventory, equipment, patents |
| **Liabilities** | Loans, accounts payable, bonds |
| **Equity** | What's left for shareholders (Assets − Liabilities) |

**Key question**: Is the company financially healthy? Does it have more assets than debt?

## 3. Cash Flow Statement

Shows how **cash actually moves** in and out of the company. A company can be "profitable" on paper but still run out of cash.

Three sections:

| Section | What It Shows |
|---------|--------------|
| **Operating Cash Flow** | Cash from core business operations |
| **Investing Cash Flow** | Cash spent on/received from investments, equipment, acquisitions |
| **Financing Cash Flow** | Cash from issuing stock, borrowing, paying dividends |

**Key question**: Is the company generating real cash from its business?

## Why All Three Matter

- **Income statement** can be manipulated with accounting tricks
- **Balance sheet** is a snapshot — it doesn't show trends
- **Cash flow** shows the real money movement

Smart investors look at all three together. A company with growing revenue but negative cash flow might be in trouble. A company with steady cash flow but growing debt deserves scrutiny.

## Where to Find Them

- **SEC filings** (10-K annual, 10-Q quarterly) at sec.gov
- Financial sites: Yahoo Finance, Google Finance, Macrotrends
- Company investor relations pages

> **Tip**: Start by reading the income statement top to bottom, then check if the cash flow statement tells the same story as net income.
